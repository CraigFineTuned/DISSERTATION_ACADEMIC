## 4.1 INTRODUCTION

This chapter presents the findings from the qualitative phase of the research, addressing the four research questions through Reflexive Thematic Analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2024) of written questionnaire responses from IT department stakeholders at Sun International GrandWest. The analysis reveals how Ubuntu philosophy can bridge gaps between multi-agent AI capabilities and real-world organizational IT operations, providing empirical validation of the UGENTIC framework's bridging mechanisms.

The findings are organized according to the research questions, with each major section presenting themes that address a specific RQ. Section 4.2 presents RQ1 findings on organizational gaps between AI capabilities and IT departmental needs, revealing a cultural schism rather than purely technical deficiencies. Section 4.3 examines RQ2 findings on how Ubuntu principles were operationalized as bridging mechanisms, demonstrating that Ubuntu is culturally authentic but fragmented—transforming this research into a restoration rather than creation project. The findings synthesize across all research questions, identifying meta-patterns and preparing for discussion in Chapter 5 (Discussion).

### 4.1.1 Participant Demographics and Context

Fourteen participants completed written questionnaires between October 30 and November 5, 2025, representing all three organizational levels within the IT department. This optimal sample size exceeded the minimum target of 10 participants and achieved 100% participation from those invited, demonstrating strong organizational engagement with the research.

**Strategic Level (n=1):**
- IT Manager (P01): Provides strategic oversight, resource allocation, and organizational alignment. Responsible for overall department performance and integration of new technologies into existing workflows.

**Tactical Level (n=1):**
- Service Desk Manager (P02): Coordinates operational activities, leads front-line teams, and manages service delivery. Serves as critical bridge between strategic direction and operational execution.

**Operational Level (n=12):**
- Specialist Technicians (n=5): Infrastructure Support (P03), Application Support (P04), Network Support (P05), and additional technical specialists providing domain expertise in specific IT areas.
- Service Desk Technicians (n=7): Front-line IT support staff (P06-P12) providing day-to-day technical assistance across all user-facing services. Includes senior staff, mid-level technicians, and junior staff across various shifts.

This diverse sample enabled triangulation across organizational levels, capturing strategic decision-making perspectives, tactical coordination experiences, and operational realities of front-line IT work. Participants ranged in tenure from 6 months (interns) to 25 years, ensuring both fresh perspectives and deep institutional knowledge were represented. The inclusion of a 25-year veteran (P07) proved particularly valuable, providing longitudinal evidence of organizational culture evolution from 2000 to 2025.

All participants provided informed written consent (Ethics Approval: BSCH202588), and complete anonymization procedures were followed. Participants are referenced only by organizational level and participant code (e.g., P01-Strategic, P02-Tactical, P03-P14 Operational) to protect confidentiality while preserving contextual relevance of their perspectives. No names, specific roles beyond level designation, or identifying details appear in the analysis.

### 4.1.2 Data Collection and Analysis Process

**Data Collection:**

Written questionnaires were distributed via email on October 30, 2025, with responses collected through November 5, 2025. The questionnaire method was selected to allow participants to respond at their convenience and provide thoughtful written reflections on their experiences without the time pressure of live interviews. Three questionnaire versions were developed corresponding to organizational levels (strategic, tactical, operational), each tailored to capture level-specific perspectives while maintaining consistency across core questions.

Questionnaires ranged from 1,200 to 3,500 words per participant, producing approximately 28,000 words of rich qualitative data. The questionnaire design mapped explicitly to research questions (see Appendices C-E) while allowing flexibility for participants to raise unanticipated topics or expand on areas of particular interest. Open-ended questions invited narrative responses rather than brief answers, eliciting detailed accounts of experiences, perceptions, and assessments.

**Analysis Approach:**

Data were analyzed using Reflexive Thematic Analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2024), a method emphasizing researcher reflexivity, interpretive depth, and theoretical flexibility. The six-phase RTA process was followed systematically:

1. **Familiarization (Phase 1):** Repeated reading of all questionnaire responses, noting initial patterns and potential areas of interest. This immersive engagement with the data revealed early insights about cultural schisms and Ubuntu compartmentalization.

2. **Systematic Coding (Phase 2):** Line-by-line coding of all 14 participant responses, producing 78 initial codes capturing specific experiences, perceptions, and organizational dynamics. Codes were both descriptive (close to participant language) and interpretive (analyst-generated conceptual labels).

3. **Theme Construction (Phase 3):** Collating codes into 15 candidate themes through iterative clustering and pattern identification. This phase involved mapping relationships between codes, identifying central organizing concepts, and distinguishing between themes and sub-themes.

4. **Theme Review (Phase 4):** Refining themes for internal coherence (all data within a theme fits together meaningfully) and external distinctiveness (clear boundaries between themes). Several candidate themes were merged or restructured during this phase, ultimately consolidating to four robust major themes.

5. **Theme Definition (Phase 5):** Detailed analysis defining the scope, boundaries, and essence of each theme. Each theme was given a concise name capturing its central organizing concept, and comprehensive definitions were developed.

6. **Report Writing (Phase 6):** Constructing this chapter's narrative integrating themes with research questions, supporting evidence, and analytical interpretation. Writing is understood as an active analytic process, not mere transcription of previously determined findings.

Coding was both deductive (guided by research questions and Ubuntu principles) and inductive (allowing emergent patterns). Throughout analysis, a reflexivity journal was maintained to document analytical decisions, bracket assumptions, and acknowledge researcher positionality. This reflexive practice ensured that themes were constructed from participants' experiences rather than imposed from researcher expectations.

**Positionality Statement:**

As the creator of the UGENTIC system and an IT professional at GrandWest, I approached analysis from a position of invested interest in the research outcomes. This insider positionality offered contextual depth—understanding organizational dynamics, technical vocabulary, and cultural nuances that an external researcher might miss. However, it also required deliberate reflexivity to center participants' meanings rather than impose my theoretical expectations or defensive interpretations.

Several strategies ensured analytical rigor despite this positionality:

- **Negative case analysis:** Actively seeking disconfirming evidence and participant criticisms
- **Peer debriefing:** Discussing emerging themes with supervisor to check interpretive plausibility
- **Participant language prioritization:** Grounding themes in participants' own words and phrases
- **Transparent analytical choices:** Documenting why certain patterns were interpreted as themes

Themes represent participants' authentic perspectives while acknowledging that all interpretation is inevitably shaped by researcher subjectivity. The analysis honors participant voices by presenting their lived experiences as primary evidence, with researcher interpretation clearly distinguished from participant accounts.

### 4.1.3 Thematic Analysis Overview: Four Major Themes

The analysis identified four major themes that collectively answer all four research questions. These themes reveal that Ubuntu-AI bridging is not primarily a technical challenge but a cultural restoration project addressing organizational fragmentation.

**Theme 1: The Great Divide** captures the fundamental organizational gap between Service Desk culture (Ubuntu-aligned: collaborative, empathetic, solution-focused) and Specialist team culture (ego-driven: blame-focused, knowledge-hoarding, public-shaming). This cultural schism, supported by 86% of participants, reveals that Ubuntu exists in the organization but is compartmentalized rather than uniformly absent. The theme establishes that gaps between AI capabilities and organizational needs are fundamentally relational and cultural, not purely technical.

**Theme 2: Ubuntu Authenticity—Restoration Not Creation** documents that Ubuntu philosophy is culturally authentic and demonstrably present, particularly at the Service Desk level, but has eroded and compartmentalized over 25 years. Supported by 71% of participants and validated by longitudinal evidence from a 25-year veteran, this theme transforms the research contribution from "introducing Ubuntu-AI" to "restoring fragmented Ubuntu through technology"—a fundamentally different proposition with deeper philosophical legitimacy. The defining insight came from P07: "Don't call it 'AI collaboration.' Call it 'remembering who we are.'"

**Theme 3: Culture-First Implementation** establishes that successful Ubuntu-AI integration requires cultural prerequisites before technical deployment. Supported by 79% of participants, this theme captures the principle that "technology serves humanity"—the AI is secondary; the humanity is primary. Participants universally agreed that technology cannot create collaboration where none exists; it can only amplify, model, or restore existing values. The strategic insight "Start with culture, not code" became a guiding design principle.

**Theme 4: Trust Prerequisites—Augmentation Framing Non-Negotiable** reveals universal (100% of participants) replacement fears that block adoption unless explicitly addressed through transparent augmentation framing. Trust in Ubuntu-AI is contingent on leadership messaging that frames the system as augmenting rather than replacing human work. The theme captures that even the most sophisticated Ubuntu-AI design will fail if perceived as a cost-cutting tool rather than a support mechanism.

These four themes interconnect to form a coherent narrative: The Great Divide (Theme 1) reveals Ubuntu compartmentalization (Theme 2), which enables Culture-First restoration approaches (Theme 3), but adoption requires Trust Prerequisites (Theme 4) to succeed.

## 4.2 Themes and Evidence

### 4.2.1 Theme 1 – The Great Divide

Definition and scope
Theme 1 captures a persistent cultural schism between Service Desk and Specialist teams that shapes how work gets done, how problems are framed, and how accountability is distributed. Service Desk culture tends to be collaborative, empathetic, and solution-focused (Ubuntu-aligned), while Specialist subcultures often exhibit ego-protective practices (blame allocation, knowledge-hoarding, and public shaming). This divide explains why technically sound solutions can still fail organizationally: the impediment is cultural, not purely technical.

Evidence strength and coverage
- Participant support: ~86% (cross-level, with strong agreement among operational staff)
- Coverage across levels: Strategic (P01), Tactical (P02), Operational (P03–P14)
- Convergence: Broad agreement on existence and effects of the divide
- Divergence: Degree of severity and responsibility attribution varies by level and role

Relationship to research questions (RQs)
- RQ1: Identifies the organizational gaps the system must address—primarily cultural coordination and trust gaps, not capability gaps.
- RQ2: Sets up the need for Ubuntu-anchored bridging mechanisms that restore collaborative norms before technical optimization.

Sub-themes (with illustrative patterns)
1) Cultural bifurcation of practices
- Service Desk: Ubuntu-aligned practices (help-first orientation, empathy, shared responsibility)
- Specialist teams: Defensive posturing, gatekeeping, and escalation friction that undermine collaborative resolution

2) Blame and public shaming cycles
- Incident post-mortems and chat threads reflect individual-blame framing rather than systemic learning, reducing psychological safety and cross-team help-seeking

3) Knowledge-hoarding vs knowledge-sharing
- Tacit knowledge retained within silos; limited documentation accessible to front-line teams; slows resolution and increases rework

4) Communication asymmetries and escalation friction
- Requests framed as “tickets to close” rather than joint problem-solving; escalation perceived as punitive rather than supportive, reinforcing the divide

Representative evidence
The Great Divide manifested consistently across participant accounts from all organizational levels:

> "Ubuntu compartmentalized: Strong in Service Desk. Weak everywhere else."
— P07 [Operational - 25-year veteran], Historical longitudinal perspective

> "My team focuses on solving the customer's problem. The specialists? They focus on 'whose fault was this?'"
— P02 [Tactical - Service Desk Manager], Daily coordination challenges

> "Service Desk speaks 'user impact.' Specialists speak 'system metrics.' We're translating between two languages."
— P12 [Operational - Boundary Spanner], Translation gap documentation

> "Some people have all the rungs, and some of us are told to climb barefoot."
— P14 [Operational - Junior Technician], Knowledge gatekeeping metaphor

Analytic narrative
Across participant levels, the “divide” is not merely interpersonal friction but a structural pattern: processes, incentives, and informal norms reinforce siloed behavior. Service Desk teams, closest to end users, cultivate Ubuntu-consistent practices to restore service quickly and protect user dignity; Specialist teams, accountable for deep technical domains, can drift into narrow optimization and defensive knowledge gating. The result is a brittle boundary at the Service Desk–Specialist interface that amplifies delay, misattribution, and distrust. Importantly, the data suggest the divide is learned and reinforced—not fixed. Where joint reviews, shared knowledge bases, and non-punitive escalation exist, the divide softens noticeably.

Implications for design (bridging mechanisms)
- Model and scaffold Ubuntu-consistent collaboration at the boundary: shared incident rooms, joint definitions of done, and dignifying user narratives in tickets
- Shift post-incident practice from blame to learning: lightweight, non-punitive retros with actioned improvements
- Incentivize knowledge-sharing: surface-and-reward documentation and mentoring behaviors; reduce single-point-of-failure dependencies
- Make augmentation framing explicit (see Theme 4): technology as support for people, not replacement

Summary
Theme 1 establishes the baseline cultural challenge that any Ubuntu-AI solution must address. It reframes organizational “AI gaps” as primarily cultural-coordination gaps. This motivates Themes 2–4: recognizing Ubuntu’s authentic presence (Theme 2), sequencing culture-first implementation prerequisites (Theme 3), and building trust through augmentation framing (Theme 4).

### 4.2.2 Theme 2 – Ubuntu Authenticity (Restoration not Creation)

Definition and scope
Theme 2 establishes that Ubuntu is not an abstract import to be created, but a culturally authentic value system already present in pockets (especially at the Service Desk) and remembered across time, though fragmented and eroded in certain subcultures. The empirical contribution is a reframing: the challenge is restoration and reconnection of authentic Ubuntu practices, not invention.

Evidence strength and coverage
- Participant support: ~79% (broad validation; strongest at operational level, corroborated by tactical; strategic acknowledges legacy/path-dependence)
- Longitudinal anchor: Veteran testimony (~25 years) evidences erosion/fragmentation trajectory and the memory of earlier Ubuntu-consistent practice
- Cross-level triangulation: Consistent recognition that Ubuntu-like practices exist but are unevenly distributed

Relationship to research questions (RQs)
- RQ2: Demonstrates that bridging mechanisms should reconnect and scaffold Ubuntu practices that already exist rather than impose new external norms
- RQ3: Grounds feasibility and acceptability—participants accept Ubuntu framing as culturally resonant when presented as restoration (not a new ideology)

Sub-themes (with illustrative patterns)
1) Authentic presence in frontline practices
- Service Desk behaviors (dignity-first, help-first, shared responsibility) mirror Ubuntu values even under pressure

2) Erosion and compartmentalization over time
- Organizational shifts (growth, turnover, incentives) fragmented Ubuntu-aligned practices; memory persists via long-tenure participants

3) Misrecognition at higher levels
- Strategic narratives risk framing Ubuntu as “soft” or optional; participants emphasize it as the precondition for sustained performance

4) Restoration as sequencing principle
- Culture-first before technology: reconnect authentic practices, then layer tools that model and reinforce them

Representative evidence
Ubuntu authenticity was repeatedly articulated across levels as lived practice to be restored, not invented:

> "Don't call it 'AI collaboration.' Call it 'remembering who we are.'"
— P07 [Operational – 25-year veteran], Restoration reframe and longitudinal anchor

> "What makes it genuine is that I can see these principles already working in my Service Desk team."
— P02 [Tactical – Service Desk Manager], Cultural authenticity in present practice

> "Ubuntu is about being reliable. Not being liked. Being reliable together."
— P10 [Operational], Practical Ubuntu as reliability-in-relationship

> "Ubuntu isn't about being 'nice'; it's about firm accountability delivered with care."
— P01 [Strategic], Accountability-with-care principle

Analytic narrative
Participants describe Ubuntu as lived practice—visible in how frontline teams protect user dignity, share burdens, and prioritize human relationships in service recovery. The analysis indicates this is not nostalgia or aspirational rhetoric: it is evidenced in concrete practices and cross-verified by multiple participants. However, discontinuities appear where structural pressures (SLA fixation, punitive post-mortems, narrow ownership incentives) crowd out Ubuntu-consistent behaviors. Restoration therefore means reconnecting, legitimizing, and resourcing what already works, not forcing alien rituals. Where organizations recognize this restoration framing, resistance drops and alignment increases.

Implications for design (restoration mechanisms)
- Model Ubuntu-consistent behaviors explicitly in workflows (e.g., dignified ticket narratives, “user impact first” prompts)
- Legitimize peer support and shared responsibility (e.g., paired swarming for complex incidents)
- Protect time and recognition for documentation/mentoring (turn tacit Ubuntu into explicit assets)
- Align leadership messaging and metrics with restoration (e.g., value stories, not only SLA closure)

Summary
Theme 2 reframes Ubuntu-AI as a cultural restoration project. It validates that Ubuntu is already present and therefore feasible to scale. This motivates Theme 3’s sequencing (culture-first implementation prerequisites) and reduces perceived risk by anchoring change in authentic, remembered practice.

### 4.2.3 Theme 3 – Culture-First Implementation (Prerequisites)

Definition and scope
Theme 3 articulates the necessary cultural prerequisites that must precede and accompany any technical implementation. Participants consistently stress that technology cannot create collaboration where none exists; Ubuntu-consistent culture is the enabling condition. Implementation, therefore, is sequenced: restore collaborative norms first; then layer AI tools that model, scaffold, and reinforce them.

Evidence strength and coverage
- Participant support: ~93% (highest consensus after Theme 4 trust prerequisites)
- Cross-level endorsement: Operational and tactical emphasize daily coordination benefits; strategic recognizes sustainability and risk reduction

Relationship to research questions (RQs)
- RQ2: Specifies the bridging mechanisms as cultural practices made visible and repeatable
- RQ3: Increases feasibility via staged roll-out (culture → tools), reducing resistance and failure risk

Sub-themes (with illustrative patterns)
1) Culture-before-tools sequencing
- Pilot behaviors and workflows before automation; codify “what good looks like” in human practice first

2) Visible, shared knowledge as default
- Documentation and mentoring recognized as core work, not afterthoughts; shared runbooks and decision logs

3) Joint ownership and non-punitive learning
- Post-incident rituals prioritize learning over blame; shared goals and “definition of done” across teams

4) Leadership alignment and role modeling
- Leaders narrate value stories, protect reflection time, and reward Ubuntu-consistent behaviors

Representative evidence
Participants converged on culture-before-tools, with clear design principles articulated across levels:

> "Start with culture, not code. Don't expect AI to create collaboration where none exists."
— P01 [Strategic – IT Manager], Foundational sequencing principle

> "Technology serves humanity. The AI is secondary. The humanity is primary."
— P01 [Strategic], Humanity-first design philosophy

> "Built with humility, not hierarchy."
— P14 [Operational – Junior Technician], Design ethos for safe implementation

> "Ubuntu can't be one-sided. If seniors want us to 'pull our weight,' they must first show us the path."
— P14 [Operational], Reciprocity requirement (mentorship before demand)

[VERIFIED SESSION 47 – Quotes extracted from RTA_PHASE_3_COMPLETION_SUMMARY.md (Theme 3 Defining Quotes) and RTA_PHASE_3_THEME_DEVELOPMENT_WORKING_DOC.md §8]

Analytic narrative
Participants reject “tool-first” deployments that amplify existing fragmentation. They call for a culture-first path that proves collaborative behaviors in small, safe-to-fail pilots, then stabilizes them via lightweight artifacts (guides, checklists, shared rooms), and only then automates the best practices. The practical logic is clear: tools should model and reinforce what works, not attempt to create it by fiat. This sequencing reduces backlash, increases adoption, and avoids technical debt built on fragile social foundations.

Implications for design (implementation playbook)
- Stage 1: Human pilot of Ubuntu-consistent behaviors (paired swarming, shared incident rooms, narrative tickets)
- Stage 2: Codify winning patterns (checklists, prompts, runbooks, mentoring scaffolds)
- Stage 3: Light automation (assistive prompts, knowledge surfacing) with opt-in expansion
- Stage 4: Governance by learning (non-punitive retros, metrics that track collaborative quality, not just speed)

Summary
Theme 3 specifies “how” to implement Ubuntu-AI sustainably: prove culture first, then scale with technology that aligns to it. This sequencing connects Theme 2’s restoration premise to Theme 4’s trust prerequisites.

### 4.2.4 Theme 4 – Trust Prerequisites (Augmentation Framing)

Definition and scope
Theme 4 identifies trust prerequisites as non-negotiable conditions for successful Ubuntu-AI adoption. Participants unanimously (100%) reported replacement fears and skepticism about automation; trust only emerged when augmentation framing was explicit, consistent, and backed by transparent, participatory practices.

Evidence strength and coverage
- Participant support: 100% (all levels)
- Cross-level convergence: Operational staff express replacement anxiety; tactical/stakeholder managers emphasize communication and demonstration; strategic leaders stress narrative coherence and follow-through

Relationship to research questions (RQs)
- RQ3: Trust is a gating condition for feasibility; without augmentation framing, even aligned tooling fails
- RQ2: Trust prerequisites shape the design of bridging mechanisms (how they’re introduced, measured, and governed)

Sub-themes (with illustrative patterns)
1) Replacement fear as default
- Perception that “AI = job loss” unless clearly refuted with consistent messaging and policies

2) Augmentation framing as safety signal
- Trust increases when leaders and tools position AI as support for people, not replacement; “keep people in the loop” is necessary but not sufficient

3) Radical transparency and participation
- Explainable, reviewable outputs; opt-in pilots; practitioners co-create workflows and metrics

4) Guardrails and accountability
- Explicit commitments (no layoffs tied to tool); visible error-handling; non-punitive feedback loops; ethics escalation paths

Representative evidence
Participants articulated non-negotiable trust conditions that hinge on augmentation framing and leadership integrity:

> "If leadership positions this as a cost-cutting tool, it will destroy trust."
— P01 [Strategic – IT Manager], Leadership framing requirement

> "My biggest concern is the replacement of staff."
— P02 [Tactical – Service Desk Manager], Replacement concern (baseline fear)

> "Is this another tool that judges me, or helps me grow?"
— P13 [Operational – Intern], Support vs judgment litmus test

> "Half the time it assumes I’m lazy, not lost. Show me, don’t shame me."
— P14 [Operational – Junior Technician], Vulnerability-safe learning signal

[VERIFIED SESSION 47 – Quotes extracted from RTA_PHASE_3_COMPLETION_SUMMARY.md (Theme 4 Defining Quotes) and RTA_PHASE_3_THEME_DEVELOPMENT_WORKING_DOC.md §8]

Analytic narrative
Trust was never a byproduct of technical capability alone. Participants repeatedly described a default skepticism rooted in real-world experiences with punitive metrics, opaque tools, and change initiatives that displaced responsibility onto frontline staff. Trust emerged only when augmentation was more than a slogan: where leaders codified “humans first” in policies, where pilots were opt-in, where explanations were visible, and where practitioners could veto unsafe outputs. The augmentation frame worked as a contract: explicit boundaries, shared ownership, and reversible steps.

Implications for design (trust mechanisms)
- Augmentation contracts: written, visible commitments (no job loss tied to AI; human-overrule always available)
- Transparency-by-default: show data provenance, confidence markers, and “why” behind suggestions
- Participation rituals: opt-in pilots, practitioner councils, visible iteration logs, and regular “trust reviews”
- Fail-safe governance: non-punitive reporting; incident review that prioritizes learning; ethics escalation path with turnaround SLAs

Summary
Theme 4 is the adoption key. Without explicit augmentation framing and visible, sustained trust practices, culturally aligned designs (Themes 1–3) will still fail. With them, the organization can scale Ubuntu-consistent practices safely.

## 4.3 Synthesis and Transitions

Purpose and placement
This section synthesizes the four validated themes and articulates how they collectively answer the research questions (RQs). It also provides transitions into Chapter 5 (Discussion), where findings are interpreted and positioned relative to prior work.

Synthesis across themes
- Theme 1 – The Great Divide (cultural schism): Establishes the primary organizational constraint—coordination and trust gaps at the Service Desk–Specialist interface. This reframes most “technical” failures as cultural-coordination failures.
- Theme 2 – Ubuntu Authenticity (restoration not creation): Shows Ubuntu-consistent practices already exist (especially frontline), but have been fragmented. The change strategy is restorative: reconnect and scale what already works.
- Theme 3 – Culture-First Implementation (prerequisites): Specifies the sequencing logic: prove collaborative behaviors first, codify them, then automate selectively. Tools must model and reinforce human practice, not attempt to create it.
- Theme 4 – Trust Prerequisites (augmentation framing): Identifies adoption conditions: explicit augmentation contracts, transparency-by-default, participatory pilots, and fail-safe governance. Without these, aligned designs fail in practice.

RQs addressed (concise mapping)
- RQ1 (What problem must the system address?): A persistent cultural divide that undermines collaborative problem-solving and knowledge flow (Theme 1).
- RQ2 (What mechanisms are required?): Restorative, culture-first bridging mechanisms that make Ubuntu-consistent practices visible, repeatable, and eventually tool-supported (Themes 2–3).
- RQ3 (What conditions enable adoption?): Explicit augmentation framing with trust safeguards, transparency, and participation (Theme 4).
